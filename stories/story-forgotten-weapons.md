## Chapter 1: Introduction

### The Table

The range was quiet except for the wind off the high desert. Ian McCollum stood behind his usual table—a six-foot folding affair, the kind scarred by decades of disassembled Mausers and mystery Austro-Hungarian pistols—but today's layout was different enough to give any regular viewer pause.

There were no serial numbers to trace. No mysterious proof marks. No patina.

The weapons on the table looked like nothing he had ever put on camera.

He turned to face the lens, a familiar posture, a familiar cadence, though the slight shift in his expression betrayed that even he was still processing the situation.

> "Hi guys, thanks for tuning in. I'm Ian McCollum, this is Forgotten Weapons, and—well. Today's episode is a little different."

He gestured to his right, where a man stood at ease in an unfamiliar uniform: clean lines, muted grey-green, no national flag on the shoulder.

> "I'm joined today by Major Christopher Reed of an organization I'm not going to name on camera, for reasons that will become fairly obvious. Major Reed, thanks for being here."

> "Thanks for having me," Reed said. He was trim, mid-thirties, with the unhurried confidence of someone who spent time around things that could kill you and had learned not to be theatrical about it. "This is, I'll admit, a somewhat unusual public relations exercise for us."

> "That might be the understatement of the channel's history," Ian said. "So—before we get to what's on this table, let's talk about you. Can you give viewers a sense of your background?"

Reed nodded, settling his weight slightly.

> "Sure. I graduated from MIT in 2019—mechanical engineering, with a minor in high-energy electrical systems. After that I went straight into the organization, and I've been with them for six years now."

> "And what does 'with them' look like, practically speaking? What does a Federation armorer's career trajectory look like?"

A beat. Reed had clearly been briefed on how much to say and had made peace with it.

> "A lot of postings. I spent the first year or so at a stateside facility, getting up to speed on our inventory—which, as you're about to see, is a fairly steep learning curve coming out of conventional engineering school. Then I rotated through several assignments. I did two years outside the Sol system, actually."

Ian kept his expression neutral, though his eyes did something.

> "Outside the—"

> "Sol system. Yes."

> "Doing what?"

> "Much the same thing any army armorer does," Reed said, with what might have been the ghost of a smile. "Maintenance, inspection, load-outs before operations, post-op assessments. Accountability for equipment. The paperwork, honestly, is not that different. The tools are fancier."

Ian looked at the camera.

> "The tools are fancier," he repeated. "Okay. He said it so I don't have to." He turned back to the table. "So. You brought some things for us to look at today. Can you walk us through what we're dealing with?"

> "Happily." Reed stepped up beside him. "I want to be clear upfront: none of this is classified in the sense that we're worried about state actors reverse-engineering it from a YouTube video. The physics are known. The *implementation* is what's proprietary. But we thought there was value in the public understanding that these tools exist and are—" he searched for the right word, "—responsibly held."

> "That's very diplomatically put."

> "I've been briefed by people who are very good at diplomatic language."

Ian gave the camera a look that said *you see what I'm working with*, and reached for the first item on the table.

> "Let's start from left to right, then. What am I looking at here?"

## Chapter 2: The Scorch

### Overview

Reed picked up the first weapon from the table with the practiced ease of someone who had handled it ten thousand times. It was compact—closer in overall dimensions to a full-size service pistol than anything exotic, though the proportions were subtly wrong in ways that took a moment to identify. The grip was slightly too deep. The barrel geometry didn't resolve into anything familiar. There was no obvious ejection port.

> "This is the *PGP-8*," Reed said, holding it up for the camera. "Code-named *Scorch*. It's our general-issue sidearm."

Ian reached for it and Reed passed it over without hesitation, muzzle angled down range out of habit rather than instruction.

> "First thing I notice," Ian said, turning it in his hands, "is there's no slide. No reciprocating mass at all that I can identify. So this is not a conventional firearm in any sense."

> "Correct. It's a plasma weapon. The mechanism that generates the bolt is internal—no moving parts in the conventional sense. What you'd normally think of as the action is replaced by a micro-scale plasma generator and a focusing column that runs the length of the barrel."

> "Okay." Ian looked down the sights. They were present, at least—a simple notch-and-post arrangement, though the front post had a small active element that cast a faint dot. "So what am I holding, energetically speaking? What does the bolt actually do when it hits something?"

> "At the power level the *Scorch* runs, you're looking at ten to fifteen megawatts," Reed said. "It's lethal against unarmored targets and lightly armored ones. Against heavier armor—vehicle-grade—you'd want a rifle."

Ian processed that for a moment with the expression of someone doing arithmetic.

> "Ten to fifteen megawatts. That's—"

> "A lot, yes."

> "And this runs on—" Ian turned it over, found what he was looking for: a rectangular block seated at the base of the grip, slightly proud of the frame. It released with a firm press of a recessed button and came free in his hand. "This is the... magazine?"

> "Thermal clip," Reed said. "That's what we call them. It's not storing chemical propellant—it's storing *heat*. The plasma generation process produces substantial waste energy, and if you let it accumulate in the weapon itself, you'll cook the internals in short order. So the clip is machined from a lithium-beryllium alloy with a very high heat capacity. It acts as a thermal sink, pulling that waste heat out of the system and holding it."

Ian held the clip up to the camera. It was featureless on most faces, warm to the touch even now. Dense, for its size.

> "So when this reaches capacity—"

> "You drop it and seat a fresh one. Same motion as a magazine change. The muscle memory transfers." Reed allowed himself a small, satisfied look. "That was intentional. Our armorers spent a fair amount of time on the ergonomics."

> "What happens to the spent clip?"

> "It cools down. If you have time to wait—a few minutes of low or no fire—it sheds the stored heat passively and is ready to go back in. You can run a rotation. Most of our troops carry two or three clips per weapon rather than a large stack, because in practice the cycle rate is low enough that passive cooling keeps pace."

Ian turned this over in his mind visibly, the way he did when encountering a mechanism that forced him to rebuild his mental model from scratch.

> "So the limiting factor isn't how many rounds you carry—it's heat management."

> "Exactly. You'll find that's a recurring theme today. Most of what we field operates on similar principles. The thermal clip system is standardized across nearly the entire infantry weapons inventory. Same interface, same alloy, different geometries depending on the weapon's thermal output."

> "That's actually elegant," Ian said, with the tone of someone who meant it as a professional judgment rather than a compliment. "Single logistics tail for the heat management side, regardless of what the weapon is actually *doing*."

> "That was the intent. Field simplicity." Reed nodded toward the weapon. "You want to shoot it?"

Ian looked at the camera.

> "Do I want to shoot the plasma pistol."

A beat.

> "Yes. Obviously."

### On the Line

Reed reached under the table and produced a hard case, compact and unmarked. He opened it toward Ian: inside, nestled in foam, sat what looked at first glance like a pair of ordinary protective eyewear—the kind any shooter would wear on a range. They were slightly too clean, the frame geometry a degree or two off from anything in a retail catalog.

> "Before you step up," Reed said, "put these on."

Ian took them, turned them over in his hands. No obvious electronics. No visible displays. He looked at Reed, then at the camera, then put them on.

For a moment, nothing. Then he raised the *Scorch* toward the line of steel targets downrange, and his expression changed entirely.

> "Oh," he said.

He didn't say anything else for a moment. He moved the pistol slightly left, then right, watching something the camera couldn't capture.

> "There's a—" he started, then reset. "Okay. There's a crosshair. It's showing me exactly where the muzzle is pointed. And there's a—" he glanced toward his peripheral vision, "—a capacity readout. I can see how much thermal headroom I have left in the clip."

> "Correct," Reed said.

> "This is just a pair of glasses."

> "From the outside, yes."

> "How is it getting the muzzle position?"

> "The weapon is broadcasting it. Short-range encrypted signal. The glasses—or a helmet display, or a head-up display in a vehicle, or a neural implant if the operator has one—receive that data and render it in the appropriate overlay format." Reed folded his hands. "It's a standard interface. Every weapon in our infantry inventory speaks the same protocol. The *Scorch*, the rifles, the heavy weapons—they all broadcast the same telemetry package. The display layer interprets it."

Ian lowered the pistol briefly, thinking.

> "So the weapon doesn't care what's receiving. It just transmits."

> "The weapon transmits. The HUD renders. They're decoupled by design—you can swap either component independently. A soldier in full powered armor with an integrated helmet sees the same information as someone in plain clothes with a pair of glasses. The experience scales to the kit."

> "And the capacity readout—that's the thermal clip state?"

> "Temperature as a percentage of saturation, yes. You always know how much thermal headroom you have. No more guessing how many shots you've fired."

Ian raised the pistol again and looked downrange. There were six steel plates at staggered distances, the kind he'd used for demonstrations a hundred times. The crosshair settled over the nearest one.

He lowered it briefly and turned to the camera.

> "By the way—what you're seeing right now in the edit, the overlay, the crosshair and the readout? That's not a post-production graphic. The Federation pulled the footage directly from the glasses and gave it to my editor. What's in the frame is what I'm actually seeing. So when I say the dot is sitting on the plate—" he raised the pistol again, crosshair steady on the steel, "—you can see that it is."

> "I'll admit," he continued, "this is doing something interesting to my sight picture. I keep wanting to line up the iron sights by reflex and then realizing I don't need to."

> "Most people take about half a magazine to stop fighting the habit," Reed said. "Then they never go back."

Ian exhaled, found his grip, and pressed the trigger.

The *Scorch* fired with a sharp, low crack—less than a gunshot, more focused, a sound with an edge to it rather than a concussion. The nearest plate took the bolt center-mass and rang with a hard metallic tone, rocking on its hanger. There was a small scorch mark where the bolt had landed, the steel slightly discolored.

He fired again. Then again. Each shot tracked exactly where the overlay told him it would. At fifteen meters, then twenty-five, then the furthest plate at nearly forty—all six in under ten seconds, the last few with the easy confidence of someone who had stopped thinking about the mechanics.

He lowered the weapon and looked at the camera.

> "Right."

A beat.

> "So this is what it's like to be really good at shooting."

Reed said nothing, but the corner of his mouth moved.

Ian checked the glasses' peripheral readout. The clip sat at roughly sixty percent saturation—a long way from spent.

> "Plenty left," he observed. "Low rate of fire, passive cooling keeping up."

> "Exactly as we discussed." Reed gestured back toward the table. "We'll come back to the HUD integration in considerably more detail later. For now—" he picked up the next weapon in the row, "—shall we move on to the rifles?"

Ian took one more look downrange at the six marked plates, then pulled the glasses off and set them on the table beside the *Scorch*.

> "Yes," he said. "Let's."

### Power

Reed stopped him before he could set the glasses down entirely.

> "Actually—keep those on for a moment. I want to show you something else the *Scorch* can do."

Ian raised an eyebrow and kept them on. Reed reached over and pressed two fingers against a small recess on the left side of the pistol's frame, near the base of the grip. Something clicked, and a faint indicator on the overlay that Ian hadn't paid attention to before—a thin bar tucked into the lower corner of the display, well below the capacity readout—began to move.

> "What just changed?"

> "Output level," Reed said. "The *Scorch* has a variable power setting. What you fired at those plates was minimum practical output—quiet, low thermal cost, enough to stop an unarmored target cleanly. I've just stepped it up one level."

Ian looked at the indicator. It had moved roughly a quarter of the way up a scale.

> "And what does one level up do?"

> "See the plate at twenty meters?"

Ian raised the pistol. The crosshair settled on the steel.

> "Go ahead," Reed said.

The crack was different this time—not louder, exactly, but harder. More *present*. The plate didn't ring. It lurched on its hanger with a grinding shriek of stressed metal, and where the bolt had landed there was not a scorch mark but a hole, the edges of the steel around it turned up and molten-bright, still glowing faintly orange as the air cooled them.

Ian stared at it for a moment.

> "That's through a steel plate."

> "Mild steel, six millimeters. Yes."

> "At setting two."

> "Of eight."

Another pause.

> "Of *eight*," Ian repeated, in the flat tone of a man choosing not to finish the sentence. He looked at the camera, then back at the smoking hole, then at the pistol in his hand. "And this is the *sidearm*."

> "Correct," Reed said, entirely without drama. "Every soldier who carries a rifle has one of these on their hip."

Ian watched the last of the orange fade from the plate's edges. The overlay in his glasses showed the thermal clip at around forty percent—the second shot had cost noticeably more headroom than the entire first string at minimum power.

> "Thermal cost goes up with output level," he observed.

> "Quadratically," Reed confirmed. "Double the power setting, roughly four times the heat per shot. At maximum output you'd get two, maybe three shots before the clip saturates. Which is why our troops are trained to treat the upper ranges as a last resort or a deliberate capability choice rather than a default."

Ian nodded slowly, processing, then set the *Scorch* back on the table with the careful deliberateness of someone who had just recalibrated their understanding of what "sidearm" meant.

> "Okay," he said. "Now we can move on to the rifles."

Reed said nothing. He was already holding one.
